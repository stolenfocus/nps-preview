What is NPS?

Korea's National Pension Service is one of the most influential institutional investors in the world. Here's everything you need to know about who they are, how they invest, and why their 13F filings matter.

The National Pension Service (NPS), known in Korean as the National Pension Fund, was established in 1988 as South Korea's mandatory public pension system. It is administered by the National Pension Service under the Ministry of Health and Welfare.

As of 2025, NPS manages over $1 trillion in total assets, making it the world's 3rd largest pension fund — behind only Japan's Government Pension Investment Fund (GPIF) and Norway's Government Pension Fund Global.

More than 22 million South Korean workers and employers contribute to the fund, which provides retirement income, disability benefits, and survivor benefits to its members.

How NPS Invests

Asset Allocation

NPS diversifies across multiple asset classes: domestic equities, international equities, domestic bonds, international bonds, alternative investments (real estate, infrastructure, private equity), and other assets. Over the past decade, NPS has steadily increased its allocation to overseas equities and alternative investments.

US Stock Portfolio

NPS's US equity holdings represent a significant portion of its international stock investments. As of Q4 2025, the fund held $135 billion in US-listed stocks across 561 positions. The portfolio is heavily weighted toward mega-cap technology stocks (NVIDIA, Apple, Microsoft, Amazon) but also includes mid-cap and small-cap positions across all sectors.

Investment Philosophy

As a long-term, liability-driven investor, NPS focuses on sustainable returns over decades rather than short-term performance. The fund employs both internal investment teams and external fund managers. NPS is known for its disciplined rebalancing approach and increasing focus on ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) factors.

What Are 13F Filings?

A 13F filing is a quarterly report required by the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) for institutional investment managers who manage $100 million or more in qualifying assets. The filing discloses the manager's equity holdings at the end of each calendar quarter.

What's Included

13F filings include the name and CUSIP of each security, the number of shares held, and the market value at quarter-end. They cover US-listed equities, American Depositary Receipts (ADRs), equity options, and convertible securities.

What's NOT Included

13F filings do not include short positions, non-US stocks traded on foreign exchanges, bonds, commodities, real estate, private equity, or cash positions. They represent only a portion of an institution's total portfolio.

Filing Timeline

13F filings are due within 45 days after the end of each calendar quarter (March 31, June 30, September 30, December 31). This means the information is always at least 6 weeks old by the time it becomes public. For example, the Q4 2025 filing (positions as of December 31) was published on February 10, 2026.

NPS CIK Number

NPS files with the SEC under CIK 0001608046. Their first filing was in November 2014 (for Q3 2014), meaning we have 46 quarters of continuous data to analyze their US stock picks.

Why Track NPS?

Scale Creates Market Impact

With $135B in US stocks, NPS's buying and selling decisions can meaningfully impact stock prices. When NPS builds a large new position, it often signals institutional-quality research and conviction that individual investors can learn from.

Professional Research Team

NPS employs hundreds of investment professionals and works with some of the world's top external asset managers. Their investment decisions are backed by deep fundamental analysis, macroeconomic research, and risk management frameworks that most retail investors lack access to.

Long-Term Orientation

Unlike hedge funds that may trade on short-term catalysts, NPS invests with a multi-decade horizon aligned with pension fund liabilities. Their stock picks tend to favor companies with durable competitive advantages and sustainable growth profiles.

Important caveat: 13F filings show a 45-day-old snapshot. By the time you see the filing, NPS may have already changed its positions. Use this data as one input among many in your research process, not as a direct signal to trade.

Our Data Sources & Methodology

Primary Source: SEC EDGAR

All NPS portfolio data comes directly from 13F-HR filings submitted to the SEC's EDGAR database. We parse the XML filing data programmatically and cross-reference CUSIPs with current ticker symbols.

CUSIP-to-Ticker Mapping

13F filings report holdings by CUSIP (a 9-character security identifier), not by ticker symbol. We maintain a mapping table that converts CUSIPs to current ticker symbols. Some older or delisted securities may not map correctly.

Quarter-over-Quarter Analysis

New buys, sold positions, and position changes are calculated by comparing consecutive quarterly filings. A "new buy" means the CUSIP appears in the current filing but not the previous one. "Sold" means the CUSIP was in the previous filing but disappeared from the current one.

Backtest Methodology

Our backtest uses actual filing dates as buy signals. We assume trades execute at the closing price on the first business day after the filing date. Returns include price appreciation only (no dividends). SPY is used as the benchmark. See the full backtest page for detailed methodology and limitations.
